Indian Rosewood (Sheesham)

Botanical Name:

Dalbergia sissoo – A fast-growing, hardy tree of the legume family native to the Indian subcontinent. It thrives in well-drained alluvial soil and is often found growing along riversides.

Common Names:

Sheesham, Indian Rosewood, Sissoo, Shisham, Tali (in various regional languages)

Description:

Indian Rosewood is a large deciduous tree reaching heights of 20–25 meters with a trunk diameter of 2–3 meters. It has compound leaves and pink to white flowers blooming in spring. The tree is known for its dense, reddish-brown heartwood with dark streaks, which makes it a prized timber.

Uses:

  • Durable and elegant furniture like beds, tables, wardrobes, and decorative carvings.
  • Sought-after wood for classical instruments including sitars, harmoniums, and tablas.
  • Ideal for watercraft construction due to its resistance to water and pests.
  • Used in luxury flooring, wood paneling, and ornamental wood veneers.

Wood Properties:

  • Density: 770–880 kg/m³ — heavy and compact
  • Hardness: Very high; resists denting and wear
  • Color: Deep reddish-brown with darker veins
  • Termite and fungal resistance
  • Excellent durability for long-lasting structures

Ecological Importance:

Sheesham trees improve soil health by fixing atmospheric nitrogen. Their shade is essential in agroforestry systems, supporting crop diversity. The tree is also effective in preventing erosion on riverbanks and highways due to its strong root system.

Cultural Significance:

Sheesham wood is revered in Indian tradition. It’s used in temples and religious ceremonies, particularly for sacred furniture and offerings. Prayer beads (japa malas) are often made of Sheesham for their symbolic strength and purity.
